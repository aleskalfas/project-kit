When a process subject is waiting, the substrate can now hold that wait as a **first-class object** — not a new state in the process definition, but an orthogonal runtime flag on the subject: what it is `blocked_on`, the condition that will `resume` it (which the engine re-checks live and clears automatically), since when, and who owns it; and for a wait on a person, the **question** being asked. The substrate already detected "this subject has no legal move" (its core derived-blocked detection); this gives that wait a name, a reason, a resume condition, an audit entry, and — for a human wait — a prompt. It is *not* a definition state, so it adds no states to any process and cannot explode the state space.

## Context

The process substrate ([COR-033](COR-033-process-substrate.md)) ships three pieces this record builds on: a transition `authorisation: user` (a move only a human may take), a **core derived-blocked detection** ("no legal move" — the engine already sees when a subject can't move on its own), and an inferred, deterministic position model. It *names but defers* (COR-033 P5) the richer human-pause / blocked capability: a first-class `blocked{blocked_on, resume_when}` flag and the question a pause poses. This record un-defers that capability.

Two bindings need more than the bare "no legal move" bit:

- The issue lifecycle (the first binding) has subjects that genuinely *wait* — an issue parked in review, blocked on a human reviewer, for an unknown duration. Operationally pm wants to know *what* is waited on, *since when*, and *on whom* — a stuck-work view the bare bit can't give.
- An adopter's trip-planning process (the second binding) has waits of two kinds: **human-decision** pauses that pose a question (prune the derived areas — which to keep?; deepen-or-proceed at saturation; intervene when a point can't clear its gates — amend the route, or accept a won't-do?), and **awaiting-the-world** pauses where verification can't complete until external data exists (an opening calendar or reservation window not yet published) and should resume *by itself* when the data appears.

Two bindings demanding the same shape meets COR-033 P5's gate (COR-007's recurrence test). This record ships the first-class blocked record — minimally, and at a deliberately bounded scope (below).

## Decision

**In plain terms:** a subject that is waiting is modelled as a first-class `blocked` flag carrying *why* it waits, *when* it resumes, *since when*, *who owns it*, and — for a human wait — *the question*; the engine re-evaluates the resume condition live and clears the flag when it holds. The flag is an orthogonal runtime annotation, **not** a state in the process definition.

A subject may carry a **`blocked` record**: `{ blocked_on, resume_when, since, assignee? }`.

- **`blocked_on` is typed**, shipping the two reasons that are establishable from *one* subject's reality: **`awaiting-human`** (a person must act) and **`awaiting-condition`** (an external fact must become true). The cross-subject reasons — **`awaiting-subprocess-outcome`** (a parent waiting on child processes) and **`deadlock`** (a mutually-blocking cycle) — are **deferred**: each can only be established by reading *more than one* subject, which is the breadth COR-032 holds back ("the engine never enumerates subjects"). They ship with the slot where the engine first gains the right to read across subjects (composition / breadth), not here.
- **`resume_when` is a predicate the engine re-evaluates live**, using the same predicate runner that already backs detection and gates. The status view shows current readiness; when `resume_when` holds the engine **clears the flag automatically**. For an `awaiting-human` wait the resume is the person taking the move; for `awaiting-condition` it is the external predicate turning true with no human in the loop. Both shipped reasons resolve from a single subject's reality, so neither stretches COR-032's single-subject engine.
- **The wait is journaled on enter and on resume** — `since` gives its age; `assignee` (optional) names its owner. The wait becomes auditable: how long, on what, on whom. The journal entries are the *audit trail* of the wait; the *current* blocked-ness is always the live `resume_when` evaluation, authoritative over any journal entry — inheriting COR-033's contract that the journal is an intent log and live detection is the source of truth.
- **An `awaiting-human` block carries a `prompt`** — the question posed to the person — surfaced on the move the status view already presents (one additive field on the existing per-move emission). This is the human-facing case: the park stops being a silent "your move" and becomes "your move: here's the question."
- **The flag is orthogonal, not a state.** It annotates a subject at its current position; it does not add a state to the process definition. Position stays inferred and the validator stays deterministic — `resume_when` is a predicate over reality, exactly like detection.

What this is **not**: it does not interpret the `prompt` or the domain meaning of `blocked_on` (content-free — the engine carries and surfaces them; the binding owns what they mean and what an answer does). It does not *fire* notifications or actions — the enter/resume **events it produces are the journal entries themselves** (no new emission mechanism); the actual dispatch is the separate deferred **hooks** slot, which reacts to those journaled transitions when it ships.

## Rationale

This is name-broad / ship-narrow ([COR-016](COR-016-scripted-scenario-storyboards.md)) applied as COR-033 P5 intended: un-defer a named slot when real bindings need it ([COR-007](COR-007-pattern-extraction.md)'s recurrence test). The bare "no legal move" bit the substrate already ships conflates three things a binding needs to tell apart — *done* (terminal, no move, fine), *idle* (between positions), and *stuck-waiting* (parked on a person or a condition, possibly for a long time). The first-class record is what makes "what is waiting, on what, since when, and what is it asking?" answerable; the bare bit cannot. Both bindings demand exactly that distinction — pm for stuck-work visibility, trip-planning for human questions and self-resolving external waits.

Modelling the wait as an **orthogonal runtime flag rather than a definition state** is the load-bearing design choice. A `blocked` *state* per pause point would multiply every process's state space (a `blocked-awaiting-X` twin of every state that can wait) and bleed waiting-mechanics into each binding's definition. The orthogonal flag keeps the definition clean and the mechanic in the engine once. It stays consistent with COR-033's deterministic-validator guarantee because `resume_when` is a predicate over reality (like detection), and position remains inferred — the flag is a derived overlay the engine re-computes, not stored truth it must keep in sync.

**The bounded scope** keeps this on the ship-narrow line even while shipping the rich record:

- **The cross-subject reasons (`awaiting-subprocess-outcome`, `deadlock`) are excluded.** Each is established only by reading more than one subject — a parent's children, or the cycle of subjects blocking each other — which is the breadth COR-032 defers ("the engine never enumerates subjects… reasoning across subjects ships when a binding needs it"). They land where the engine first gains cross-subject reading (the composition / breadth slot), so all cross-subject mechanisms converge in one place rather than leaking in one reason at a time. This record declares only the two reasons establishable from a single subject's reality.
- **The hooks (notify-on-enter / act-on-resume) firing mechanism is excluded** — it is its own named-deferred slot. This record produces the enter/resume events *as journal entries*; the hooks slot reacts to those journaled transitions later.
- **A structured `selection` option-set is excluded.** A free-text `prompt` carries the question; whether the engine should also carry (and validate) a fixed option-set is a second thing — it raises a content-freeness question (validate the answer against the options, or opaque passthrough?) and not every human wait has a fixed option-set ("which areas to keep?" is an open choice over runtime-derived areas). The prompt clears the recurrence; `selection` un-defers when a binding needs engine-carried options.

### Alternatives considered

- **Ship only the prompt; keep the whole blocked record deferred.** Rejected by the maintainer's scope decision — the bare "no legal move" bit cannot answer "what is waiting, on what, since when, on whom," which both bindings need (pm's stuck-work view; trip-planning's self-resolving `awaiting-condition` waits, which have no human move for a prompt to ride). The prompt alone serves only `awaiting-human`; the first-class record serves the wait as such.
- **Model `blocked` as a definition state (a `blocked-awaiting-X` state per pause).** Rejected — it multiplies every process's state space and pushes waiting-mechanics into each binding's definition. An orthogonal runtime flag keeps definitions clean and the mechanic central.
- **Ship the cross-subject reasons (`awaiting-subprocess-outcome`, `deadlock`) now too.** Rejected — both are establishable only by reading more than one subject (a parent's children; the blocking cycle), which is the breadth COR-032 defers. `awaiting-subprocess-outcome` also depends on composition's parent↔child machinery that does not yet exist; `deadlock` would force the engine to enumerate and traverse subjects it is forbidden to enumerate. Shipping either now would either be inert or grow a cross-subject mechanism ahead of the slot that should own it. They ship where the engine first reads across subjects.
- **Ship the hook-firing mechanism with the blocked record.** Rejected — notify/act dispatch is a distinct named-deferred slot; the blocked record need only expose the enter/resume events it will react to.
- **Ship a structured `selection` option-set alongside the prompt.** Rejected for this slice — a second addition beyond the question, raising a content-freeness question; a binding can render options in the prompt text until a later slice carries them as data.

## Implications

- **Shape contract.** Adds an orthogonal optional `blocked` record on a subject (`blocked_on` ∈ {`awaiting-human`, `awaiting-condition`}, `resume_when` predicate, `since`, optional `assignee`) and an optional `prompt` on a `user`-authorisation transition. Both are additive — existing processes carry neither and are byte-unchanged — so no migration is owed (per [COR-010](COR-010-resource-lifecycle.md)), given the status view's consumers tolerate additive fields (the standard for its structured/`--json` surface). The `blocked_on` enum is open to additive widening — the deferred cross-subject reasons join it when their slot ships. The exact field layout lives in the process-area shape reference, not here.
- **Engine.** The engine gains: detecting entry into `blocked` (building on the shipped core "no legal move" detection), live `resume_when` re-evaluation through the existing predicate runner, automatic clearing when it holds, journaling on enter/resume, and surfacing the record (and an `awaiting-human` block's `prompt`) on the status view. Position stays inferred; the validator stays deterministic. This is a surface change; the affected backbone component's version bumps per the project's version policy when the work lands.
- **Acceptance-gated implementation.** This record decides the rule; the schema widening and engine work are downstream tasks that may not proceed until this record is accepted.
- **Grounding bindings carry their own instances.** pm annotates its review/stuck waits (`awaiting-human`, with owner/since for the stuck-work view); the adopter's trip-planning process authors its human-question pauses (prune-areas, gate-failure, deepen-or-proceed, visit-placement) and its `awaiting-condition` availability waits — once the slot ships, so the engine slot and a real binding land together and the slot is never inert.
- **Adjacent capabilities remain deferred**, each its own future decision when a binding needs it: the **cross-subject `blocked_on` reasons** (`awaiting-subprocess-outcome` and `deadlock`, shipping where the engine first reads across subjects), the **hooks** firing mechanism (notify-on-enter / act-on-resume), and a structured **`selection`** option-set.
- Relationship to records: implements the human-pause / blocked slot named in COR-033 (P5), building on its core derived-blocked detection; applies name-broad / ship-narrow (COR-016) and extract-on-recurrence (COR-007); does not amend COR-033 — un-deferring a named extension point is fulfilment, not revision (mirrors COR-032).
