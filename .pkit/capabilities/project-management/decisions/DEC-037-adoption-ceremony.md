## Context

[project-management:DEC-036-substrate-pluggable-adoption] and its read-path contract (ADR-026) make a brownfield repo *readable*: at runtime the capability resolves each conceptual axis (type / priority / workstream / lifecycle-state / hierarchy) through the adopter's `substrate-map.yaml` and degrades per-axis where a substrate is absent. That is the **passive read side** — "given a map, how does pm resolve an axis." Both records deliberately scope OUT the **write side of adoption**: the one-time and ongoing *ceremonies* that put the adopter's corpus into a state pm can operate on, and that keep new issues conforming to adopter-specific conventions pm would never ship.

The grounding case (AUJ) makes the gap concrete. Read-mapping alone leaves three needs unmet:

1. **Drafting the map and the ceremonies from a live repo.** DEC-036 assumes a `substrate-map.yaml` already exists, but a brownfield adopter starts with none — and hand-authoring it against hundreds of existing issues is error-prone. DEC-036 named an **`adopt-existing`** tool and deferred it.
2. **Back-filling the existing corpus (one-time).** AUJ wants every existing issue's Projects-v2 `workstream` field set to **`Spyre`** (an adopter-specific value, not a kit vocabulary), and all issues assigned a **time-based milestone**. That is a bulk transform over hundreds of real, human-owned issues.
3. **Defaulting new issues going forward.** Every *new* issue should seed `workstream=Spyre` (and any other adopter default) without the filer restating it each time.

None of these is a runtime read-resolution, so none belongs in the substrate-map's read path. They are **mutations of adopter state at adoption time and on each create** — the same family as [project-management:DEC-017-prerequisites-bootstrap-migrate-discipline]'s `bootstrap` (additive setup) and `migrate` (state reconciliation), but with a brownfield twist: the corpus is already full of human-owned data, so the load-bearing concern is *not breaking it*. Bulk-editing hundreds of real issues silently is the failure mode this record must rule out.

## Decision

**In plain terms:** adopting a brownfield repo is a guided ceremony, not a silent rewrite. pm can *inventory* the live repo and *draft* (never auto-apply) a substrate-map plus the population scripts; it can *propose* a one-time corpus back-fill as an auditable, human-confirmed report (never a silent mass-edit); and it can *seed* adopter-default attribute values onto every new issue through the write path DEC-036 already established. Adopter-specific population logic and reusable presets are named here and ship when a real binding needs them.

### 1. `adopt-existing` — inventory-and-draft, never auto-apply

The capability gains an **`adopt-existing` ceremony** (the tool DEC-036 deferred). It is the brownfield analogue of [project-management:DEC-017]'s `bootstrap`: where greenfield `bootstrap` *creates* the kit's own substrate additively, brownfield `adopt-existing` *reads the existing substrate and drafts the binding*. It:

- inventories the live tracker (labels in use, title-prefix patterns, open/closed + blocked conventions, Projects-v2 fields, milestone usage);
- **drafts** a candidate `substrate-map.yaml` (and, where the adopter wants population, draft back-fill scripts) for the human to review and edit;
- **never writes** the map or mutates the repo on its own. Output is a draft artifact + an audit report of what it observed and inferred.

The draft-not-apply discipline is the whole point: the inferred binding is a *hypothesis about the adopter's conventions*, and a wrong inference applied silently would mis-map an entire corpus. The human owns acceptance.

**Honest bound on what draft-not-apply buys.** It guards the *map-write* — the human never gets a silently-installed binding. It does **not** make the map and the later back-fill report two *independent* safety layers: the back-fill's propose-and-cite report (point 2) is computed *through* the accepted map, so a mis-inferred-but-rubber-stamped map yields a wrong-but-plausible back-fill report that the human is also liable to rubber-stamp. The two acceptance gates share the inferred map as a **common-mode input**; they are one root inference confirmed twice, not defence-in-depth. The mitigation is to make the `adopt-existing` audit report show *why* each binding was inferred (the evidence in the corpus), so the human's acceptance is informed rather than reflexive — not to claim the second gate independently catches a bad map.

### 2. Corpus back-fill — auditable propose-and-cite, never a silent mass-edit

A one-time bulk transform over the existing corpus ("set `workstream=Spyre` on all N issues; assign milestone X to the set") belongs to [project-management:DEC-017]'s `migrate` family — **not** the substrate-map. It runs through the **auditable propose-and-cite report** posture: enumerate the proposed per-issue changes, cite why each is proposed, the human confirms (per-change or per-reviewed-batch), and only then does it apply. Four safety properties make "auditable" load-bearing rather than decorative:

- **non-silent** — no bulk mutation of human-owned issues without a reviewed plan presented first; the report is the gate, mirroring DEC-017's per-change confirmation discipline for destructive migration;
- **re-validate at apply (closes the report→apply window)** — the report enumerates issue state at plan time, but the human reviews for minutes-to-hours, during which humans may edit issues concurrently. Each per-issue mutation **re-reads that issue's current state immediately before applying** and, if it has drifted from the enumerated plan, **skips and reports the skip** rather than overwriting against stale enumeration. The plan is a proposal, not a committed write set;
- **idempotent by per-issue value-equality** — "already done" is detected by **value-equality on the target attribute** (this issue's `workstream` field already equals `Spyre`; this issue's milestone already equals X), not mere presence. A re-run after a partial apply (an interrupted bulk loop that died at issue K) is therefore a no-op for the already-applied issues and completes the rest — partial-apply is recoverable. This is per-issue grain, finer than DEC-017's manifest-entry-grain `migrations-applied.yaml` tracking, because the unit of human-owned state here is the individual issue;
- **gated on the residual hard-fail subset of `pre-check`** — DEC-036 made `pre-check` *degrade to a capability matrix* in brownfield mode rather than hard-refuse, so the gate is not "any pre-check failure." It is the subset that still hard-fails in brownfield: `gh` auth invalid, repo inaccessible, or the `substrate-map.yaml` fails to parse. Those break the plan's assumptions and refuse the back-fill; a merely-degraded axis does not.

A **`--emit-script` mode** is supported as the draft-not-apply symmetric form: instead of pm applying on confirmation, it emits the reviewed mutations as an idempotent script the adopter runs themselves at their chosen moment. This makes the back-fill symmetric with the `adopt-existing` ceremony (both can draft-not-apply) and hands the apply-timing to the human, for adopters who prefer pm never touch their corpus directly.

This is the design-sensitive part of the record: bulk-mutating real, human-owned issues is the highest-blast-radius operation pm performs, and these properties — not the bare "we show a report" — are what make it acceptable.

### 3. Per-axis defaults for new issues — split by substrate kind

Defaulting a new issue's attribute is a **per-axis `default:`** declared once in the `substrate-map.yaml` alongside that axis's binding (DEC-036 already carries per-axis `default:`). **How** the default is emitted depends on what substrate the axis is bound to, and the two cases must not be conflated:

- **Label-bound axes** (a `type:*` / `priority:*` / `workstream:*` label set the adopter owns): the default emits on the write path through ADR-026's **sole-constructor seam** — the same `resolve_write` / `axis_default` resolution #265 shipped. Here it genuinely *is* "no new mechanism": the default is the value the seam emits when the filer supplies none, and the seam is already the sole constructor of axis labels.
- **Non-label substrates** (the AUJ grounding case — `workstream` is a **Projects-v2 field**, not a label; milestones are a third substrate again): the label seam does **not** construct these, and an axis bound to a field (or marked `unsupported` for label purposes while still carried on a field) **fail-closes through `resolve_write` to a dropped label** — so a `default:` on such an axis cannot reach the corpus through the label seam at all. Seeding a Projects-v2 field value or a milestone on a new issue requires a **field/milestone write step** in `create-issue` — the *same* write primitive the corpus back-fill (point 2) uses, applied per-create instead of one-time. This is a real (small) new mechanism on a substrate distinct from labels; the record does not pretend otherwise. ADR-026's sole-constructor invariant is **label-scoped** and is not violated — the field write is a different substrate path with its own (single) construction point.

So the honest unification is: there is **one adopter-default-value capability** with two emission sites — the label seam for label-bound axes, and a field/milestone write step for non-label substrates — and that same field/milestone write step is exactly what the one-time back-fill (point 2) drives over the existing corpus. AUJ's `workstream=Spyre` travels the non-label path, both as a new-issue default and as a back-fill.

**This is an invariant, not an aspiration.** ADR-026 learned (its own ~26-site refactor) that "single construction point" stated as intention but unenforced is *provisioned, not structural*. The field/milestone write path is born with the same exposure unless pinned: **one module is the sole constructor of each non-label substrate write** (Projects-v2 field value, milestone assignment); **both drive sites — the per-create default and the one-time back-fill — route through it**; and a guard analogous to ADR-026's holds it, so a future author cannot re-scatter the construction by string-formatting a field mutation inline in a third script. This contract is architecturally significant and cross-consumer; it lands in a **sibling ADR — a write-path contract for non-label substrates, parallel to ADR-026's read-path/label contract** — authored at implementation time once this record settles (the same way DEC-036 spun its read-path contract out to ADR-026 rather than inlining it). ADR-026's sole-constructor invariant stays label-scoped and intact; this is its write-side, non-label twin.

### 4. Population hooks and presets — named, ship-narrow

- **Population hooks** — the adopter-supplied logic that *computes* the values a back-fill or a default uses (which milestone, the `Spyre` constant, any repo-specific rule). pm ships a **hook slot the adopter fills**; the adopter authors the content. This relates to the deferred **`hooks`** process-substrate slot parked in EPIC #237 — named here as the carrier, not built in this record beyond what the AUJ back-fill demands.
- **Presets** — common brownfield adopter shapes (a ready-made map + back-fill for a recognised tracker convention) are **named-deferred** per [COR-007](../../../decisions/core/COR-007-pattern-extraction.md): harvest a preset from real adopters once the same shape recurs, rather than speculating a catalogue now.

### Carrier discipline — this is a pm-local DEC

This record settles the **pm capability's** adoption ceremonies and stays pm-local. It must **not** name or define the generic shared bootstrap/adoption shape across capabilities. Brownfield adoption is plausibly a cross-capability pattern (living-docs, EPIC #234, will face the same "inventory an existing corpus, propose an auditable transform" need), but the shared shape is **COR-promotable only on the second instance**, extracted from pm (this record) *and* living-docs once both exist — the same defer-until-second-instance discipline DEC-036 and [project-management:DEC-022-methodology-mesh]'s "capability-local at v1, COR-promote on the second instance" applied. Naming the generic shape here, from one instance, would invent inventory ahead of recurrence.

**Be precise about *which* shape defers.** Two shapes are in play and only one is the thing to extract. The propose-and-cite **report *format*** ("enumerate changes, cite why, human reviews, then apply") is thin and arguably already recurs (permissions `diagnose`, an IGW docs audit) — but it is cheap, convergent, and not worth a COR. The load-bearing shape is the **auditable bulk-mutation-of-live-human-owned-state *safety contract*** — the four properties named authoritatively in point 2 above, which exist because the corpus is hundreds of *concurrently-editable, human-owned* records. permissions `diagnose` does not face that (it proposes over config the kit largely owns); pm's back-fill is its **first** instance, and living-docs #234 the plausible **second**. So the deferred-until-second-instance target is the *safety contract*, not the report format — a future reader must not extract the thin thing on a report-format head-count. When it does extract, it composes with [project-management:DEC-017]'s own pre-declared "a future COR may generalise this pattern to all capabilities that mutate external state" — DEC-017 is the seed of the eventual cross-cutting COR (the `pre-check`/`bootstrap`/`migrate` safety-profile triad), and this record's `adopt-existing` + auditable back-fill *add evidence to that same seed*, they do not found a parallel concern.

## Rationale

**Why a ceremony record separate from the read-path records.** DEC-036/ADR-026 answer "how does pm resolve an axis through the map at runtime." This record answers "what one-time and ongoing mutations run when a brownfield repo is adopted." Conflating them would put high-blast-radius write operations (bulk corpus edits) under a contract written for read-only resolution. The two surfaces have different safety profiles and deserve different records — the same separation DEC-017 drew between read-only `pre-check`, additive `bootstrap`, and destructive `migrate`.

**Why draft-not-apply for `adopt-existing`.** An inferred substrate-map is a *hypothesis* about conventions a human established implicitly. Applying it silently and then operating on a mis-inferred binding would corrupt the adopter's read of their own corpus. Inventory + draft + human-acceptance keeps the human as the authority over what their conventions mean — and matches `bootstrap`'s additive-only humility (it never modifies existing state) on the brownfield side.

**Why the back-fill must be auditable, not silent.** This is the load-bearing safety call. A brownfield corpus is hundreds of human-owned issues; a silent bulk `gh` mutation across them is irreversible-in-practice and opaque. The propose-and-cite report — enumerate, cite, confirm, apply — is the same posture pm already trusts for destructive `migrate` (DEC-017's per-change confirmation) and that the permissions capability uses for its `diagnose` report. But the inheritance is at the **safety-profile** level (both destructive, both need confirmation, both idempotent), not the **correctness** level: DEC-017's `migrate` reconciles *kit-introduced* drift and can validate its result against the new schema (the label it renamed to is in the schema); this back-fill writes *adopter-specific* values (`Spyre`, a chosen milestone) for which **pm has no schema to validate against**. Value-correctness is therefore the **adopter hook's** responsibility (point 4); pm guarantees only the *shape* properties — non-silent, re-validate-at-apply, value-equality idempotency, residual-pre-check gate. The record states this split rather than letting "same family" imply pm checks the values are right. And because per-change confirmation that protects three issues becomes a rubber-stamp wall at corpus scale, the practical gate is the **reviewed batch** (or `--emit-script` + the adopter's own apply), not per-issue clicks.

**Why defaults split by substrate kind.** For a label-bound axis, a default value is just "what the write path emits when the filer supplies none," and ADR-026 already made the seam the single place axis *labels* are constructed — so emitting a declared default there is the minimal addition and a parallel label constructor is exactly what ADR-026 forbids. But the grounding case (AUJ `workstream`) is a Projects-v2 *field*, which the label seam never constructs and which `resolve_write` fail-closes to a dropped label; pretending the field default "reuses the seam" would be false. The field/milestone write is a genuinely distinct substrate path — and the right move is to make it *one* path shared between the per-create default and the one-time back-fill, so there is still a single construction point per substrate, just not the *label* one.

**Why hooks and presets are named, not built.** COR-007's pattern-extraction discipline: build the slot the AUJ binding demands (a hook the adopter fills for the `Spyre`/milestone logic), and defer the catalogue of presets until a second adopter shows the recurring shape. Shipping a preset library from one grounding case would be inventory ahead of recurrence.

### Alternatives considered

- **Put back-fill in the substrate-map (read path).** Rejected — the map is a runtime read-resolution contract (ADR-026); a one-time bulk write over the corpus has the opposite safety profile and belongs to the `migrate` family.
- **Auto-apply the inferred substrate-map.** Rejected — a mis-inferred binding applied silently mis-maps the whole corpus; the human must own acceptance of what their conventions mean.
- **Silent bulk back-fill with a single `--yes`.** Rejected — defeats the audit gate on the highest-blast-radius operation pm performs; mirrors DEC-017's rejection of a batch `--yes` on `migrate`.
- **pm-applies-on-confirm only (no draft-a-script form).** Not chosen as the *sole* form — the `--emit-script` draft-not-apply mode is also supported, making the back-fill symmetric with `adopt-existing` and handing apply-timing to the human (which also closes the report→apply window for adopters who use it). pm-applies remains the default for adopters who want pm to drive.
- **Claim the field/milestone default "reuses the seam, no new mechanism."** Rejected as dishonest — the seam constructs labels only; AUJ's `workstream` is a Projects-v2 field the seam fail-closes. The field/milestone write is named as a real (small) new substrate path, shared between per-create default and one-time back-fill.
- **Define the generic cross-capability adoption shape now.** Rejected — first-instance-stays-local (DEC-022 / DEC-036 precedent); the shared shape extracts on the second instance (living-docs #234), not from one.
- **Ship a preset catalogue from the AUJ case.** Rejected — inventory ahead of recurrence (COR-007); presets harvest from real adopters.

## Implications

- **A new `adopt-existing` ceremony** joins the capability's script family (`pre-check` / `bootstrap` / `migrate` per DEC-017). It is read-and-draft: inventory + draft map + draft population scripts + audit report; it mutates nothing. Same-PR-as-surface-change discipline (DEC-017) applies — it is part of the capability surface and bumps the capability `version:`.
- **The corpus back-fill** ships as a `migrate`-family operation governed by the propose-and-cite report posture: enumerate → cite → **re-validate per-issue at apply (skip+report drift)** → apply, with **per-issue value-equality idempotency** and a **`--emit-script`** draft-not-apply alternative. It gates on the residual hard-fail subset of `pre-check` (auth / repo-access / map-parse), not on degraded-axis checks (DEC-036 made those degrade). Value-correctness is the adopter hook's responsibility; pm guarantees the shape/audit properties. The concrete write primitives crystallise as the AUJ binding needs them, per COR-007 — see the next point for which AUJ demands now.
- **A field/milestone write primitive is in-scope now, not deferred** — AUJ's grounding need (`workstream` on a Projects-v2 field, time-based milestone) is a non-label substrate the label seam does not construct, so the back-fill and the new-issue default both require it. This record fixes the *requirement* — it must be the **sole constructor** of non-label substrate writes, with both drive sites (one-time over the corpus; per-create in `create-issue`) routing through it and a guard holding the invariant — and defers *formalising and enforcing* that contract to the sibling ADR below.
- **ADR follow-up** — the write-path contract for non-label substrates (sole-constructor pin + guard + the four back-fill safety properties as write-path invariants) lands as a **sibling ADR to ADR-026**, authored by the architect at implementation time once this record is accepted (per COR-025; the architect owns `<adr-records>`). This record names the follow-up; the ADR carries the contract — mirroring how DEC-036 spun its read-path contract out to ADR-026.
- **`create-issue`** emits per-axis `default:` values **two ways by substrate kind**: label-bound axes through ADR-026's sole-constructor seam (`resolve_write` / `axis_default`, shipped in #265); non-label substrates (Projects-v2 field, milestone) through the field/milestone write step above. The substrate-map schema's per-axis `default:` (DEC-036) is the single declaration point for both; no schema change beyond what DEC-036 already carries. ADR-026's sole-constructor invariant stays label-scoped and intact.
- **A `hooks` slot** for adopter-supplied population logic is named here and relates to the deferred process-substrate `hooks` slot (EPIC #237). pm ships the slot shape the AUJ back-fill demands; the adopter authors the content. Anything beyond that binding's demand is deferred.
- **Presets** are named-deferred; no catalogue ships in this record.
- **Carrier:** this DEC stays pm-local. The generic cross-capability adoption/back-fill shape is COR-promotable only once living-docs (#234) is the second instance; this record must not pre-empt that extraction.
- **Supersession:** none. This record extends the brownfield adoption arc (DEC-036 / ADR-026) onto the write side; it does not supersede them or DEC-017 (it specialises DEC-017's `bootstrap`/`migrate` family for the brownfield corpus).
