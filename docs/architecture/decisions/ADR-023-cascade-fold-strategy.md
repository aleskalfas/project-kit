## Summary

[COR-037](../../../.pkit/decisions/core/COR-037-process-cascade.md) decided the *rule* — a parent process may look across **all** the subjects of **one** named child process that belong to it and fold their outcomes into one answer that gates the parent; the fold ships narrow (`all` / `count`, child → parent, fail-closed). This ADR records the *engine strategy* built to honour that rule, in `src/project_kit/process.py` ([ADR-020](ADR-020-process-engine-code-home.md)'s engine home), on branch `feat/204`. It is the on-call/maintainer reference for how the cross-subject fold actually works and how it fails. It is the **sibling [ADR-022](ADR-022-subprocess-resolution-strategy.md) explicitly forecast** ("cascade will *consume* this single-inner resolution as its per-subject step, not replace it"): where ADR-022 records resolving **one** inner outcome (DEPTH-1, single subject), this ADR records folding **many** member outcomes (BREADTH over a finite set, still depth-1 per member). Same machine, one axis wider.

**The strategy, in one breath:** the engine never holds a containment tree. To learn which subjects to fold, it asks the binding through **two** predicates — a content-free `members` source (run once, threaded with the parent subject, returning the parent's candidate ids) and a single-subject `membership` test (run one candidate at a time, threaded with **only** that candidate's subject). The two predicates do *different* jobs and only the `members` source is parent-scoping: the `members` source is the **sole parent-scoping authority** (it is the only predicate the engine threads the parent through, so it is the only one that *can* answer "which subjects belong to this parent"), while the `membership` test is a per-candidate **membership-class re-confirm** (it cannot re-scope to the parent — the engine never hands it the parent — it re-confirms the candidate is still a live member of the relation's *class* and turns an unreadable candidate into a fold-hold). For each confirmed member it resolves the member's terminal outcome through the **same** `_resolve_inner` helper composition uses (so there is one cross-process path, not two), then folds the resolved outcomes with the declared reducer (`all` = every member reached outcome X; `count` = at least N did). The fold is **fail-closed on partial indeterminacy at two places**: an unresolved member *outcome* and an indeterminate *membership* test each hold the gate shut rather than letting an `all` vacuously pass. The *empty set* is the third place where the fold resolves on no members, but it is **no longer unconditionally fail-closed** — it resolves by the binding's declared `on_empty` policy (`fail-closed`, the default, keeps the gate shut; `satisfied` opens it), always *determinately*. The fold feeds an engine-computed `cascade-outcome` gate, and the parent's `awaiting-cascade-outcome` wait auto-clears the instant the fold opens.

Three things make this fold sound rather than a reopening of [COR-032](../../../.pkit/decisions/core/COR-032-keyed-process-subjects.md)'s never-enumerate line. The engine reads across subjects only through **one declared child relation scoped to one parent subject**, and only by running a binding-supplied predicate **one subject at a time** — it never receives or sweeps a global subject list. Each member outcome is itself a composed definite-answer-over-reality (ADR-022's single-inner resolution), so [COR-033](../../../.pkit/decisions/core/COR-033-process-substrate.md) P3 still holds — no position is stored or synthesized. And the fold adds **breadth** (set size) not **depth** (resolution nesting): it does not recurse a member's own subprocess/cascade gates, so resolution stays single-level and terminating, inheriting ADR-022's `_resolution_stack` acyclicity guard — keyed on the **(address, subject) pair** so a parent folding sibling subjects of its **own** process resolves (the common keyed-fold case), while a same-`(address, subject)` self-embed is still refused (see section 2).

## Context

COR-037 settled the rule but explicitly deferred the *how* to acceptance-gated implementation, naming the architecturally-significant choices it left open: "which side enumerates (settled here: the binding, via predicate), how the fold is engine-computed, and the partial-indeterminacy fail-closed semantics … warrant a sibling ADR authored when engine work begins." That implementation has now landed (branch `feat/204`) and introduces machinery with cross-cutting weight future maintainers and on-call engineers need recorded: the **first** place the engine reads across a crowd of subjects (crossing the line every prior P5 slot held), a new candidate-set seam, and a new fail-closed failure taxonomy at the cross-subject boundary.

ADR-022 is the immediate sibling: it decided how the engine resolves *one* inner outcome across a process boundary (the DEPTH axis, single-level). This ADR decides how the engine folds *many* member outcomes across the same boundary (the BREADTH axis) and sits at the same altitude — it stands *on* ADR-022's single-inner resolution rather than beside it.

The decision space has four architecturally-significant choices, each with a plausible alternative the implementation rejected:

1. **Which side enumerates, and through what seam** — a two-predicate split (`members` source + `membership` test, both binding-supplied) vs. a single membership predicate, an engine-held containment tree, or a general subject-listing API.
2. **How each member's outcome is obtained** — reuse COR-036's single-inner resolution per member (the shared `_resolve_inner`) vs. a second, cascade-specific cross-process path.
3. **How the fold reaches the parent's decision** — an engine-computed `cascade-outcome` gate vs. a capability predicate reporting the fold or a synthesized parent position.
4. **What fails closed vs. what is a correct wait** — the partial-indeterminacy failure taxonomy at the cross-subject boundary (the load-bearing new shape).

## Decision

**In plain terms:** the engine answers "did every member of this parent's named child reach outcome X?" by asking the binding *which* subjects are candidates and *whether each one* belongs (one subject at a time, never a list it sweeps), resolving each member's outcome through the very same machine that resolves a single subprocess, and folding the results — refusing to open the gate if any member's outcome or membership is unresolved, or if there are no members at all.

### 1. The two-predicate seam — the binding enumerates, the engine folds

The architecturally-significant new field shape is that the cascade declaration carries **two** predicates, not one. `resolve_cascade_outcome` obtains the parent-scoped candidate set from a content-free **`members`** predicate (run **once**, threaded with this parent's subject, returning `{members: ["id", ...]}`), then re-confirms **each** candidate through a single-subject **`membership`** predicate (run one candidate at a time, threaded with **only** that candidate's subject id). The engine never holds or discovers a containment tree; it asks the binding for this parent's candidates and tests them one at a time.

**Where parent-scoping lives — the seam contract, stated exactly.** The two predicates are *not* two halves of one "does this subject belong to this parent?" question; they answer different questions, and only one of them can see the parent. The engine threads the parent's subject through **the `members` source alone** (`_cascade_candidates` runs it on `self.runner`, the parent's runner); it threads the `membership` test through a **fresh per-member runner carrying only the candidate's subject id** (`_cascade_member_belongs`), never the parent. So:

- **`members` is the sole parent-scoping authority.** It is the only predicate that receives the parent, so it is the only one that *can* answer "which subjects belong to *this* parent?" — and it does: it returns exactly the candidate set scoped to this parent. Parent-faithfulness lives here and only here.
- **`membership` is a per-candidate membership-class re-confirm — structurally *not* a parent-scoping check.** With only the candidate's subject in hand and no parent to compare against, a single-subject predicate *cannot* re-scope to a parent; it has nothing to scope against. Its two load-bearing jobs are (a) **liveness** — catching a candidate whose membership has since changed or been removed between the `members` read and the fold (the relation's class membership re-read live, per-candidate), and (b) **fail-closed symmetry** — turning an *indeterminate* read of a candidate into a whole-fold hold rather than a silent candidate-drop (the C1 decision in section 3). It confirms the candidate is still a live member *of the relation's class*; it does not, and cannot, confirm it belongs to *this* parent.

**Why two predicates, not one — the load-bearing seam.** A single `membership` predicate alone would force the engine to obtain a universe of subjects from *somewhere* and sweep it asking "is each one a member?" — and the only universe available is the global subject list COR-032 forbids the engine to hold. The `members` source exists precisely so the *binding* supplies the bounded candidate set (it knows its own containment; the engine does not), and the per-subject `membership` test then re-confirms each candidate through the existing single-subject runner — the same content-free, one-subject-at-a-time crossing COR-032 holds the line at. Splitting source from test is what lets the engine fold across subjects *without ever enumerating* them itself: the binding enumerates its own candidates; the engine only ever folds and only ever re-confirms one subject at a time. The `members` predicate is the candidate-set seam, mirroring how detection gets its inputs — content-free and binding-supplied.

**Consequence a second binding must know — the `members` source is load-bearing for correctness, not just for enumeration.** Because `membership` cannot re-scope to the parent, an *over-broad* `members` source is not caught downstream: if `members` emits a subject that does not belong to this parent, `membership` will (correctly, by its own contract) confirm it as a live member of the class and the engine will fold its outcome — there is no second check that would exclude it. A binding's `members` predicate is therefore the one and only place that decides *whose* outcomes the fold sees; it must be exactly parent-scoped. (The pm closure binding gets this right: `cascade_members` emits only children whose body parent-ref names the folding parent, while `cascade_membership` re-reads the same parent-ref merely to confirm the child still declares *a* parent — a liveness re-read, not a parent match. A future binding — trip-planning's POI → area — must scope its own `members` predicate to the area with the same care; it cannot lean on `membership` to narrow an over-broad area read.)

### 2. The fold reuses COR-036's single-inner resolution — no rival path

Each confirmed member's terminal outcome is resolved by the **same** single-inner resolution composition uses: `_resolve_member_outcome` builds a synthetic embedding `{runs: <child address>, subject: <member id>}` and runs it through the shared **`_resolve_inner`** helper — the identical code path `resolve_subprocess_outcome` calls for a parked subprocess state. There is no second, cascade-specific cross-process mechanism. This is the "designed once" claim COR-037 made ("the mechanism is designed once, here, for cross-subject *fold* needs … future aggregation routes through this declared relation rather than growing a rival fold path") realised in code: cascade is the consumer, composition the foundational unit, and they share one seam.

Because the per-member step *is* the single-inner resolution, cascade inherits its properties for free: single-level depth (each member is read at depth 1 — its detected position, not its own subprocess gates), and the `_resolution_stack` **acyclicity guard**, keyed on the **(address, subject) PAIR** (not the address alone). This keying is load-bearing for the most common cascade shape: a parent whose `cascade.runs` is its **own** process address, folding over its **sibling subjects** (the synthetic per-member embedding `{runs: <own address>, subject: <member id>}` carries the SAME address as the parent but a DIFFERENT subject M≠N). That is a legitimate breadth fold, **not** a cycle — the per-member step reads the member's terminal position single-level and never recurses the member's own cascade — so it must resolve. (pm's closure cascade is exactly this: `issue-lifecycle` folds over its child issues, which are themselves `issue-lifecycle` subjects.) The guard refuses **only** a member at the SAME `(address, subject)` as a stack entry — a true self-embed (subject N folding over its own subject N) that would recurse infinitely. Keying on the address alone would refuse the whole same-process fold (`P -> P`), which was the PR #212 regression that broke same-process container closure; the pair-keyed guard is the PR #213 fix. **Cascade adds breadth across a finite member set, never depth.** Resolution terminates by the same bounded-depth argument ADR-022 records; cascade widens the axis from one subject to N, not from one level to many.

### 3. Fail-closed partial-indeterminacy — and the empty-set policy

The fold is fail-closed at every point where the answer cannot be soundly *established*, and the new shape relative to ADR-022 is *partial* indeterminacy — one member out of many being unresolved must hold the whole fold, never silently shrink the set. Two of the three terminal conditions below (an unresolved member outcome, an indeterminate membership test) are pure fail-closed: the answer cannot be established, so the gate stays shut and the fold reports `indeterminate`. The third — the empty set — is different in kind: the answer *can* be established (there are determinately zero members), so it resolves *determinately* by the binding's `on_empty` policy rather than failing closed unconditionally:

- **An unresolved member OUTCOME** (still moving, parked, indeterminate, or a cyclic self-embed the inherited guard refused) holds the fold `indeterminate` — the gate stays shut, never a false "all reached X." The member's own resolution reason is surfaced so a distinct cause is visible on the fold.
- **An indeterminate MEMBERSHIP test** (the `membership` predicate errored / timed out for a candidate) **also** holds the fold `indeterminate` — *symmetric* with an unresolved member outcome. This is the load-bearing C1 decision: an indeterminate membership is **not** a silent candidate-drop. Dropping it would look like "fewer members" and could let an `all` vacuously pass; instead the gate stays shut until the membership of every candidate is determinate. A determinate `result is False` cleanly excludes a *real* non-member (that is not indeterminacy — it is a confident "not a member").
- **The EMPTY set** (no candidates, or candidates existed but none were members — the two intentionally still collapse) resolves by the binding's declared **`on_empty`** policy, and is **determinate either way** — never vacuously true, never indeterminate. The policy is a closed two-value choice on the cascade declaration: **`fail-closed`** (the default, and absent) holds the gate shut (`opened=False`) — an area with zero points-of-interest is not "fully discovered," a confident "not yet"; **`satisfied`** opens it (`opened=True`) — "nothing to wait on" (project-management's childless-container closure). Both are a determinate answer; the policy only chooses which way the determinate answer *points* — `indeterminate` stays `False` in both directions, and only `opened` flips. The reducer is **short-circuited on the empty set** (`if total == 0` returns before `all` / `count` is evaluated), so `on_empty` governs *both* reducers uniformly — it forecloses a `count` floor of `0` opening the gate by its own vacuous arithmetic, exactly as it forecloses an `all` vacuously passing. This is **purely additive**: absent ⇒ `fail-closed`, so an existing cascade declaration is byte-unchanged and no migration is owed (COR-037 amended).

**Precedence — indeterminate membership / enumeration ≻ `on_empty`, by control-flow ordering, not a new conditional.** The empty-set policy can only resolve `satisfied` (open the gate) on a set that is *determinately* empty — and that guarantee is enforced structurally, not by an extra guard inside the empty branch. The broken-enumeration early-return (the `members` source was indeterminate) and the in-loop indeterminate-membership early-return (the C1 guard above) both fire *before* the empty-set branch (`if total == 0`) is reachable. So a broken read that confirms zero members never enters the empty-set branch at all — it has already returned `indeterminate`, gate shut. `satisfied` therefore **cannot fail-*open* on a broken read**: a wholesale membership-read failure is not an empty set, it is indeterminacy, and indeterminacy holds the gate shut regardless of policy. The ordering *is* the precedence rule.

A malformed cascade declaration (incomplete fields, invalid reducer op/outcome, a `count` with no integer threshold) fails closed `indeterminate`, surfaced like an unrecognised gate kind. The reading `cascade-outcome` gate maps `opened` → pass, `indeterminate` → refuse-and-name-why; a determinate-not-yet-opened fold simply does not pass (the parent correctly waits) and is *not* indeterminate.

### 4. The `cascade-outcome` gate and the aggregate wait — acyclic by construction

The fold reaches the parent's decision through a new engine-computed **`cascade-outcome`** gate (`_evaluate_cascade_gate`), **mirroring** the `subprocess-outcome` kind (which itself mirrors `authorisation-artifact`): the engine computes `result` from its own machinery rather than trusting a predicate, because the fact gated is one only the engine can establish soundly. The gate carries no `predicate` and no per-gate `outcome` (the named outcome lives in the reducer); the schema enforces that shape. This keeps one seam, not a third mechanism.

The parent's **aggregate wait** — COR-036's deferred "many-inner wait" — is un-deferred here as the `awaiting-cascade-outcome` blocked branch. It auto-clears like `awaiting-subprocess-outcome` (no `resume_when`; the live fold *is* the condition, re-evaluated through `has_no_legal_move`): it clears the instant the fold resolves open and a legal move exists. **It is acyclic by construction** — the parent waits only on its members' *already-terminal* outcomes, and a terminal subject waits on nothing, so the aggregate wait cannot join a wait cycle. This is *why* COR-034's deferred `deadlock` reason is safe to leave deferred here: cascade's wait graph cannot produce one.

### Boundaries — what this strategy is NOT

- **Outcome-fold, child → parent only.** It folds members' *terminal outcomes* into a gate. Forward/position cascade (bumping a parent to match its furthest child, over non-terminal positions) is a different machine and stays capability-local (COR-037). Overflow/hand-off (a terminal state unblocking a concurrent peer) is the orchestration altitude and is deferred.
- **`all` / `count` only.** Two reducers, one machine. Richer reducers (ratios, weighted, custom) stay deferred until a binding demonstrably needs one.
- **Breadth, not depth.** It widens single-inner resolution across a finite member set; it never recurses a member's own subprocess/cascade gates. The engine stays single-level.
- **Not the home for every cross-subject concern.** Peer-subject wait cycles (`deadlock`, COR-034) and cross-subject invariants (COR-035) are *different* cross-subject shapes that stay separately deferred. This slot is the home for cross-subject *folds* only.

## Rationale

The central tension COR-037 named is that this is the **first** place the engine reads across a crowd of subjects — it most stretches COR-032's never-enumerate line. The two-predicate seam resolves that tension by *never letting the engine enumerate at all*: the binding supplies its own bounded candidate set (it owns its containment), and the engine only ever tests and folds one subject at a time. A single membership predicate would have forced the engine to sweep an externally-held universe — the exact global list COR-032 forbids — so the source/test split is not ergonomic sugar, it is the structural reason the line stays held while the fold becomes possible. The crossing is *necessary* (the fold must open a parent gate, and gates are engine-evaluated — a fold the engine cannot read cannot gate a parent), and it is held to the minimal, declared, predicate-fed form.

Reusing `_resolve_inner` per member (rather than a cascade-specific cross-process path) is the "designed once" discipline made real: composition was built as the foundational unit precisely so cascade could consume it. One shared seam means the acyclicity guard, the single-level depth bound, and the P3 inferred-from-reality guarantee all transfer to cascade *for free* — there is no second place for them to drift. A rival fold path would have had to re-derive all three and could have diverged on any of them.

The fail-closed taxonomy is the consistency choice extended to *partial* indeterminacy. ADR-022 fails closed on a single inner being unresolvable; cascade's new shape is that *one of many* being unresolved must hold the *whole* fold. Two conditions are genuine fail-closed (an unresolved member outcome, an indeterminate membership test); the third, the empty set, is now resolved *determinately* by the binding's `on_empty` policy (COR-037 amended) rather than failing closed unconditionally — but it is held safe by the same fail-closed instinct, because the precedence rule routes any *broken* read into indeterminacy before the empty-set policy can ever apply. Treating an indeterminate membership symmetrically with an unresolved outcome (the C1 decision) is the load-bearing call: the cheap-but-wrong alternative — drop the candidate the membership predicate could not classify — silently shrinks the set and can let an `all` pass over a universe the engine never fully saw. Symmetric loudness at the COR-032 seam is what keeps the fold honest.

Mirroring `subprocess-outcome` (and through it `authorisation-artifact`) for the gate kind keeps the engine-computed-gate surface coherent: this is now the third instance of "a gate the engine computes because only the engine can establish the fact soundly," and it takes the same form rather than inventing a fourth mechanism (COR-007's recurrence applied to the gate-kind taxonomy).

### Alternatives considered

- **A single `membership` predicate, no `members` source.** Rejected — it forces the engine to obtain a universe of subjects to sweep, and the only universe available is the global subject list COR-032 forbids. The two-predicate split lets the binding enumerate its own candidates while the engine only ever tests one at a time.
- **An engine-held containment tree** (the engine discovers and caches parent → child membership). Rejected — it makes the engine hold cross-subject structure, dissolving COR-032's line, and stores state the stateless engine must keep synced against live reality. The live-re-read predicate pair keeps membership a determinate-at-the-instant read, never stored.
- **A general cross-subject enumeration / subject-listing API.** Rejected — it over-ships past COR-032 and reopens arbitrary cross-subject reading. The declared, bounded, predicate-fed one-relation fold crosses the line without dissolving it.
- **A second, cascade-specific cross-process resolution path** (instead of reusing `_resolve_inner`). Rejected — it grows a rival fold path COR-037 explicitly designed against, and forces the acyclicity guard, depth bound, and P3 guarantee to be re-derived where they can drift. The shared helper makes cascade a true consumer of composition.
- **Silently drop a candidate whose `membership` test is indeterminate.** Rejected — it conflates "could not classify" with "confirmed non-member," shrinking the set and letting an `all` vacuously pass over subjects the engine never resolved. Holding the whole fold indeterminate (symmetric with an unresolved outcome) is the fail-closed answer.
- **Treat the empty set as vacuously true** (`all` over no members opens the gate *as a matter of logic* — the universally-quantified statement is vacuously satisfied). Rejected — an area with zero points-of-interest is not "fully discovered"; the engine does not read the empty fold as logically vacuously-true. **This rejection still stands, and `satisfied` does not re-litigate it.** The later `on_empty: satisfied` mode (COR-037 amended) is **not** the vacuous-truth reading returning under a new name: it is an *orthogonal, determinate binding-policy choice* about which way a determinately-empty set should point for *this binding*, not a claim that the `all` reducer is vacuously true. The two are different axes — **policy direction** (does an empty container mean "nothing outstanding, proceed" or "not-yet-started, wait?") versus **logical vacuity** (is `∀x∈∅ P(x)` true?). `satisfied` answers the first by binding intent and short-circuits the reducer entirely (the empty set never reaches `all` / `count`); the rejected alternative answered the second by logic and let the reducer pass vacuously. A future reader must not collapse them: the engine never opens the gate on logical vacuity; it opens it only when a binding has *declared* that its empty case is satisfied.
- **Expose the fold via a capability predicate** rather than an engine-computed gate. Rejected — the fold is the engine's own designed-once machinery; a predicate would re-implement it outside the engine or trust a capability to report a cross-subject aggregate. One engine-computed gate kind keeps a single seam.

## Implications

- **Engine control flow.** `src/project_kit/process.py` gains the cross-subject fold path (`resolve_cascade_outcome` → `_cascade_candidates` (the `members` source, once) → per-candidate `_cascade_member_belongs` (the `membership` test) → `_resolve_member_outcome` → shared `_resolve_inner`). It is the first cross-subject read in an engine that acted on one subject until now; it stays single-level (each member read at depth 1) and inherits the `_resolution_stack` guard, now keyed on the **(address, subject) pair** (PR #213) so a same-process fold over sibling subjects resolves while a same-`(address, subject)` self-embed is still refused.
- **The fold is a standalone backbone read — `pkit process cascade`.** `resolve_cascade_outcome` is intentionally exposed as its own backbone CLI command (`pkit process cascade <address> [--subject …]`, `src/project_kit/cli.py`), not only as an internal step of gate evaluation. A wrapper can therefore read the fold **independently of a cascade-gated transition** and compose it — AND it — with a gate of its own. This is the structural home for the **conjunctive-eligibility pattern** [project-management:DEC-034](../../../.pkit/capabilities/project-management/decisions/DEC-034-cascade-slot-binding.md) grounds: pm's close-eligibility is *the checkbox gate AND the children-fold* — two gates of **different kinds** ANDed into one decision. The engine has no AND-of-two-gate-kinds primitive (a transition carries one gate kind; the engine evaluates that one kind and reports pass / refuse), so the conjunction necessarily lives in the **wrapper**, and the wrapper needs the fold as a standalone read to be one of its conjuncts. The command is **read-only** and exits non-zero unless the fold is open, so a wrapper can branch on it cleanly. This affordance is a **durable, content-free primitive** of the cascade strategy, not a pm-specific escape hatch: any binding whose eligibility is "this fold AND something else" reads the fold this way (trip-planning's "all POIs verified AND the itinerary is locked" is the next anticipated consumer). The engine stays the single source of the fold; the conjunction stays in the binding that owns the *composite* rule.
- **Gate-evaluation surface.** `precheck_transitions` branches on `kind == "cascade-outcome"` to `_evaluate_cascade_gate` (engine-computed) alongside the existing `subprocess-outcome` branch; `deterministic` / `authorisation-artifact` are unchanged. A `cascade-outcome` gate carries no `predicate` and no `outcome` (schema-enforced); the named outcome lives in the `cascade.reducer`.
- **Blocked overlay.** `evaluate_blocked` gains an `awaiting-cascade-outcome` branch reusing `has_no_legal_move` (no `resume_when`; auto-clears), the aggregate wait COR-036 deferred. Its audit colour (`_cascade_wait_reason`) re-runs the live fold for a human-readable `reached/total` status line; the live decider remains `has_no_legal_move`. The wait is acyclic by construction (the parent waits only on members' already-terminal outcomes).
- **Failure-mode contract for on-call.** Two fail-closed conditions hold the gate shut and name why: an unresolved member outcome, and an indeterminate membership test (NOT a silent drop — symmetric with the outcome case). A malformed cascade declaration also fails closed. The empty set is **not** a failure — it resolves *determinately* by the binding's `on_empty` policy. **An empty cascade with `on_empty: satisfied` *opens* the gate, and that is correct, not a bug.** If you see a parent advance on a childless container (e.g. project-management closing an empty container), that is the declared `satisfied` policy firing, not a fail-open — do not "fix" it. The one thing that *would* be a bug is a `satisfied` cascade opening on a *broken membership read*; that cannot happen, and the precedence rule is why: an indeterminate `members` source or membership test returns `indeterminate` (gate shut) *before* the empty-set branch is reachable, so a broken read holds the gate shut even under `satisfied`. If a `satisfied` cascade is *not* opening on what looks like an empty set, suspect an indeterminate membership read (check the `members` / `membership` predicate) — the engine is correctly refusing to treat a broken read as empty. A determinate-but-not-yet-opened fold (`all` not all reached / `count` floor not met / `fail-closed` empty set) is a *correct wait*, not a failure — wait, or check the member that has not finished; do not debug the engine.
- **Known limitation (accepted).** No per-render fold memoisation. `resolve_cascade_outcome` is invoked 2–3× per `status` render (precheck gate + `position.cascade` surface + the blocked wait-reason) × N members, and each member's outcome and membership resolve through a fresh, uncached runner — so member predicates re-run per call. Accepted for the narrow ship (the bindings' member sets are small); a shared per-render fold cache is deferred until a binding's set size makes it pay.
- **Additive, no migration.** A process declaring no `cascade` / `cascade-outcome` gate / `awaiting-cascade-outcome` wait is byte-unchanged and unaffected; existing definitions validate as before (COR-010 — no migration owed). The pm closure rebind onto this shared shape is COR-037's pre-budgeted migration, scoped to closure (not pm's forward cascade).
- **Relationship to records.** Records the engine strategy for COR-037 (the rule); sibling to ADR-022 (single-inner resolution, the DEPTH axis this extends along BREADTH) and ADR-020 (engine code-home). Preserves COR-033 P3/P6 (each member outcome a composed definite answer; the fold a deterministic reduction over a finite set, single-level and terminating). Holds COR-032 (the engine never enumerates — it folds across one declared relation, one subject tested at a time, via the binding-supplied `members` source). Un-defers COR-036's many-inner aggregate wait for this one declared relation. Keeps COR-034's `deadlock` safely deferred (the aggregate wait is acyclic by construction). Reuses the `subprocess-outcome` engine-computed-gate precedent for the `cascade-outcome` kind. This is the **last** of the four COR-033 P5 slots — blocked (COR-034), invariants (COR-035), composition (COR-036), cascade (COR-037) are all shipped.
